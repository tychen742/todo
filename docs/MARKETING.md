# Marketing

## Core Positioning

**Simple and useful** — immediately helpful on day one, without training, setup, or configuration overhead. Adopted in minutes and kept because it genuinely helps.

## The Simplicity Insight

Most work — in most jobs, on most projects — is not complicated. Day-to-day operations are routine, repetitive, and often bordering on mundane. The problem is not that people need more powerful tools; it is that the tools they have are far more complex than the work itself.

ClickUp, Asana, and Notion were designed for the most complex possible project. Real users spend most of their time on tasks that need one sentence, a due date, and a person responsible. Everything else is overhead.

This app is built on the opposite bet: **start with the simplest thing that is genuinely useful, and add only what earns its place.**

## What That Means in Practice

- A todo is text, a priority, and optionally a due date. That is enough to be useful.
- A project is a planning container for todos. No nested folders, no custom fields, no views to configure.
- A phase tells you where you are in the project. Tap to move forward.
- A milestone is just a todo that represents a real commitment — a demo, a launch, a hand-off.
- Critical path is not a Gantt chart. It is: which todos are overdue before the next milestone?

## The Consultant Opportunity

Consultants and freelancers are a high-value acquisition channel. A single consultant working with three clients does not just represent one user — they represent a potential entry point into three separate organizations.

The dynamics work in our favor:

- A consultant joins, creates an org for each client engagement, and invites client contacts to collaborate on project todos and deliverables.
- Each client contact experiences the product through the consultant's workflow.
- When the engagement ends or the client wants to keep using the tool on their own, they already know it — the conversion cost is nearly zero.
- A consultant who hits the 3-org free limit has a natural, non-punitive reason to upgrade. They are not blocked; they understand why paying makes sense.

This is a consultant-led growth model: the consultant does the work of introducing the product to multiple organizations simultaneously.

**Consultant pitch:** "Manage every client in one place. Personal todos for your own work. A separate organization for each client with their own team, projects, and phases. Switch between clients in seconds. Start free for up to three clients."

**What to build to serve consultants well:**

- Fast org switching from the account menu (already in place)
- Clear visual separation between orgs and their teams
- Project closure with a lightweight summary — consultants need proof of delivery
- Org-scoped branding so each client's workspace can feel distinct

## Target Message by Audience

**Personal users:** "Your todos, without the clutter."

**Small teams:** "Assign it, track it, ship it — without the kickoff meeting about your project management tool."

**Academic / research groups:** "Deadlines, assignments, and project phases — built for how academic work actually flows, not how enterprise software imagines it does."

**Small businesses:** "The project tool that does not require a project manager to set up."

**Consultants and freelancers:** "Manage every client in one place. One app, separate orgs, no context switching chaos."

## What Not to Say

- Do not compete on feature count. ClickUp has more features. That is the problem this app solves.
- Do not say "all-in-one." That is what makes tools complicated.
- Do not promise AI automation, integrations, or dashboards until those things are genuinely useful in the product.

## The Project Kanban Story

Most teams reach for a Kanban board the moment work gets too big for a list. But most Kanban tools come loaded with swim lanes, WIP limits, story points, velocity metrics, and sprint ceremonies — tooling invented for large engineering organizations and grafted onto everyone else.

Our take: **a project is a backlog plus a sequence of phases.**

- Backlog is a one-line strip at the top. Tasks land there by default — quick capture, no friction.
- Below that: phase columns (Planning, Execution, Review, or whatever the team names them). Each column shows its date range so everyone knows the schedule at a glance.
- Moving a task from Backlog into a phase is how planning happens. No sprint ceremonies required.
- Add a new phase with one tap. Max five phases: scope creep lives in the phases, not the tooling.

This is the first Kanban experience that does not require a Kanban expert to set up.

**For marketing:** Lead with "Backlog to delivery in three columns." Show the phase date ranges. Show a milestone task with its countdown. That is the whole pitch in one screenshot.

## The Honest Pitch

Most project management tools are built for the 5% of projects that are genuinely complex: enterprise software rollouts, construction bids, government contracts. They make the other 95% harder.

This app is built for the 95%.
